I spent an embarrassing amount of time implementing "gutter quotes" or "continued quotations" style quotations for this site.  Lorum ipsum Official Vatican Report Exposes Major Cracks in Foundation of Traditionis Custodes Previously undisclosed documents raise serious questions about the stated rationale for Pope Francis’ 2021 decree restricting the Traditional Latin Mass.

So for the TL;DR show me the code? My actual implementation (for now, sadly, only for <BLOCKQUOTE>’s) is on GitHub, in JavaScript (and associated CSS).

So, what exactly do I mean by "gutter quotes" or "continued quotations"?

Best to show some examples of this style in the wild:

Physica Sacra

Physica sacra, 1731-1735:


Physica sacra is Johann Jakob Scheuchzer’s masterpiece and magnum opus — a strange, wonderful, and beautiful set of books—exceeding 2,000 pages and containing more than 750 engraved copperplates—exquisitely typeset.  The text is mostly Latin, but also includes French, German, Hebrew, and Greek.  The typesetting and printing of the Physica sacra was overseen by the publisher, Johann Andreas Pfeffel, the Augsburg-based Hofkupferstecher (lit. "court copperplate engraver") to the Imperator Romanorum, Holy Roman Emperor, Charles Ⅵ, a major patron of the arts and sciences.

Absolutely every aspect of the layout and typography is practically perfect—to the letter (pun intended)—all the way down to microtypographical nuances like interword-spacing used when justifying the text, leaving no "rivers" of whitespace or other visual flaws, although they had not yet begun to systematically adopt more advanced layout techniques like character protrusion (i.e. margin kerning).

While primarily employing indented italicized block-quotes, as you can see in the image above, when the continued quotation style is used in the second or right-hand column, they place the quotation marks on the right but not outside the margin.

In the quotation style used for this site, I’ve implemented this right-hand style as well, although currently limited to two-column layouts.

Just how would you tackle this style in a three-column layout, with the quotation in the middle column? Would you place the marks on both sides of the text?

For a bit more details about the work itself, you should read Stephanie Stillo’s Bound Images: Information Networks in Johann Jakob Scheuchzer’s Physica sacra (1731-1735)

A New Account of East-India and Persia

A New Account of East-India and Persia, 1698:

The above except comes from the 1698 printing of John Fryer’s A New Account of East-India and Persia, or more properly, "A New Account of East-India and Persia, in Eight Letters.  Being Nine Years Travels, Begun 1672.  And Finished 1681.  Containing Observations made of the Moral, Natural, and Artificial Estate of Those Countries: Namely, of their Government, Religion, Laws, Customs.  Of the Soil, Climates, Seasons, Health, Diseases.  Of the Animals, Vegetables, Minerals, Jewels.  Of their Housing, Cloathing, Manufactures, Trades, Commodities.  And of the Coins, Weights, and Measures, Used in the Principal Places of Trade in Those Parts."

This copy was provided by the University of Oxford Bodleian Library and digitized by Google.  Other complete versions of this work are available online from the Heidelberg University Library and the Qatar Digital Library.

The wonderfully extended and notated Hakluyt Society 1909 edition is also available online, in three volumes: Ⅰ, Ⅱ, Ⅲ.  This "modern" edition is, unfortunately, much less interesting typographically, as it eschews the traditional letterforms and ligatures.  It does, however, retain the continued quotation style:

A New Account of East-India and Persia, 1909:

It should be noted that throughout "A New Account", the primary way that quotations are typeset is the familiar indented block-quoted style.  However, when that style is not used, all other quotes use the continued quotation style exclusively.

Typographical Antiquities, 1749

Typographical Antiquities, 1749:

The above excerpt comes from the 1749 (1st edition) printing of Joseph Ames’ "Typographical Antiquities", or more properly,"Typographical antiquities; Being an Historical Account of Printing in England: With some Memoirs of our Antient Printers, And A Register of the Books Printed by Them, From The Year ⅯⅭⅭⅭⅭⅬⅩⅪ [1471] to the Year ⅯⅮⅭ [1600].  With An Appendix Concerning Printing in Scotland and Ireland To the same Time.  By Joseph Ames, F.R.S.  And Secretary to the Society of Antiquities.  London: Printed by W. Faden, and sold by J. Robinson, in Ludgate-Street.  ⅯⅮⅭⅭⅩⅬⅠⅩ [1749]."

These books are an indispensable trove of insight for enthusiasts of early print and typography.  Besides the advertised content (which is more bibliographical than historical), the intricate frontispiece, beautiful mezzotints, and engraved plates, these volumes are an excellent reference for the evolution of various stylistic conventions in typography, language, and page layout over the seventy-year span (1749 - 1819) that the various editions were released.

Typographical Antiquities, 1180-1819

… and most famously, the four volume 1810-1819 Dibdin edition: "Typographical Antiquities; or the History of Printing in England, Scotland, and Ireland: Containing Memoirs of our Ancient Printers, And A Register of the Books Printed by Them.  Begun by the late Joseph Ames, F.R.  and A.SS., considerably augmented by William Herbert, of Cheshunt, Herts; And now greatly enlarged, with Copious Notes, And Illustrated with Appropriate Engravings; Comprehending the History of English Literature, and a View of the Progress of the Art of Engraving, in Great Britain; by the Rev. Thomas Frognall Dibdin."

As explained by the Cambridge Library Collection:

When this work was published, its original author had been dead for fifty years.  As the title page explains, the work of Joseph Ames (1687-1759) was considerably augmented by William Herbert (1718-1795), and then ‘greatly enlarged, with copious notes, and illustrated with appropriate engravings’ by Thomas Forgnall Dibdin (1776-1847).  […] Ames’ history of printing, based on his own collection, was published in 1749, as an aid to booksellers in identifying old works (and modern forgeries).  Herbert, a printseller and bibliophile, acquired Ames’ own interleaved copy of the work and intended to enlarge it, but died having completed only three of six proposed volumes.  His working copies passed to Dibdin, who eventually published this four-volume edition between 1810 and 1819.  […]

It’s interesting (but disappointing, at least to me!) to see that after the 1749 edition, the use of the continued quotation style was dropped, changing to double quotes in 1785 and then switching to single quotes throughout, in the 1810 edition:

Typographical Antiquities, 1785:

By the 1810 edition, the ſ (long ſ) has disappeared and "ct" ligatures are gone, though many "classical" forms still remain (i.e. "Coūntry", "Jmprinted").
